Introduction

Suicide remains one of the largest public health concerns in the US. Claiming 40,000 lives every year, suicide ranks as 10th leading cause of death.

The CDC has tracked suicide rates for generations, but what does the data really tell us? Is this issue growing? Is it preventable?


Let's take a look at deaths by suicide across the US.

How is this data broken down between genders?

An area graph reveals a significant disparity between males and females. In 2017, 66,472 male suicide deaths were reported while 6,963 female suicide deaths were reported. Why is there a tenfold increase from females to males?









Perhaps the answer lies in the method by which the death occurs. Examining data from the CDC provides some insight into the preference of violent methods in males.








A thorough look at age groups, genders, and several decades reveals a worrisome trend in the past 30 years among a certain age group in particular- 35-54.









While the issue of suicide casts a great shadow over our country, it is one often dealt with in solitude. It is important that this data is evaluated in order to search for solutions in which healthcare and outreach can be improved for those suffering from depression.
